## For FRCP 12(b)(6) Motion to Dismiss, US District Court (3rd Circuit)

| Claim Type | SOL Period | Statute |
|---|---|---|
| Tort / negligence / personal injury | 2 years | 42 Pa.C.S. § 5524 |
| Fraud / deceit / misrepresentation | 2 years | 42 Pa.C.S. § 5524(7) |
| Medical malpractice | 2 years + 7-year repose | 42 Pa.C.S. § 5524(2) |

## II. The Discovery Rule — When the Clock Starts

Pennsylvania's default accrual rule starts the clock on the date of injury — not the date of discovery. However, the **discovery rule** tolls the SOL where the plaintiff was reasonably unaware of both the injury AND its causal connection to the defendant.

**Fine v. Checcio, 870 A.2d 850 (Pa. 2005):** Established the objective "reasonable diligence" standard. The question is not what plaintiff actually knew, but what a reasonably diligent party SHOULD have known. Whether this standard was met is ordinarily a jury question.

**Gleason v. Borough of Moosic, 15 A.3d 479 (Pa. 2008):** Reinforced that accrual under the discovery rule is a fact question at summary judgment absent undisputed facts. At 12(b)(6), this creates a high bar for defendants — dismissal fails unless the complaint affirmatively shows the plaintiff knew of injury AND cause but delayed filing.

**Practical implication for 12(b)(6):** Unless the complaint affirmatively establishes that the plaintiff knew (or had constructive notice) of both injury and causation on a specific date, and that date is more than the SOL period before filing, dismissal will be difficult. The SOL bar must be "apparent on the face of the complaint."

## III. Tolling Doctrines

### A. Fraudulent Concealment
- Requires an **affirmative independent act of concealment** by the defendant that caused plaintiff to relax vigilance
- Mere silence is insufficient; defendant must affirmatively mislead or deceive
- Intent to deceive NOT required — unintentional deception suffices
- Same reasonable diligence standard applies: plaintiff cannot benefit from fraudulent concealment if her own lack of diligence prevented discovery

### B. Equitable Tolling (General)
- Third Circuit: plaintiff must plausibly allege facts supporting tolling, not merely assert it (*Cunningham v. M&T Bank Corp.*, 3d Cir. 2016)
- Even affirmative defendant misconduct does not toll the SOL if plaintiff lacked reasonable diligence
- Bare assertions of tolling are insufficient under Twombly/Iqbal

### C. Minority Tolling
- 42 Pa.C.S. § 5533: SOL tolled during minority (under 18)

## IV. 3rd Circuit Standard: SOL Dismissal at 12(b)(6)

**Robinson v. Johnson, 313 F.3d 128, 135 (3d Cir. 2002):** Affirmative defenses (including SOL) may be raised via 12(b)(6) only when they present an "insuperable barrier to recovery" **apparent on the face of the complaint.** Defendant bears the burden.

**Wisniewski v. Fisher, 857 F.3d 152, 157 (3d Cir. 2017):** Confirmed the "apparent on the face" standard. Accrual date must be mathematically derivable from the complaint's own allegations without extrinsic evidence.

**Cunningham v. M&T Bank Corp. (3d Cir. 2016):** Plaintiff must plausibly allege tolling facts — federal pleading standard applies to tolling claims.
