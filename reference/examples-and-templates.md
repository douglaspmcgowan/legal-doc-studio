# Example Motions to Dismiss — SOL Grounds, PA Federal Courts
## Argument Templates and Case Precedent

---

## I. Controlling Precedent for Granted SOL Dismissals (3rd Circuit / PA Districts)

### Robinson v. Johnson, 313 F.3d 128 (3d Cir. 2002)
- **Significance:** The baseline rule. SOL may be raised at 12(b)(6) only when the defense is "apparent on the face of the complaint."
- **Applied:** Defendant established from the complaint itself that the claim accrued outside the limitations period.

### Wisniewski v. Fisher, 857 F.3d 152 (3d Cir. 2017)
- **Claim:** § 1983 civil rights (prisoner)
- **SOL:** 2 years (borrowed from PA personal injury SOL)
- **Outcome:** Granted dismissal; accrual date was unmistakably disclosed by complaint's own allegations
- **Key holding:** Reaffirmed "apparent on the face" standard; court need not look beyond complaint to find the bar

### Pierce v. Montgomery County Opportunity Board, Inc., 884 F. Supp. 965 (E.D. Pa. 1995)
- **Claim:** Employment discrimination
- **Outcome:** Dismissed as time-barred; complaint's factual allegations clearly disclosed the discrimination occurred outside the limitations period
- **Rationale:** SOL bar "apparent on the face of the pleadings"

## II. Standard Argument Template for SOL-Based 12(b)(6) Motion

### Section: "I. PLAINTIFF'S CLAIMS ARE BARRED BY THE STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS"

**Step 1 — State the applicable SOL:**
> "Plaintiff's [claim type] claim is governed by [applicable PA statute or borrowed SOL]. Under Pennsylvania law, [claim type] claims must be brought within [X] years of accrual. 42 Pa.C.S. § [section]; *[supporting case]*."

**Step 2 — Establish accrual date from complaint's own allegations:**
> "A cause of action accrues when the plaintiff has a right to institute suit — i.e., when the plaintiff suffers harm attributable to the defendant's conduct. *Fine v. Checcio*, 870 A.2d 850, 857 (Pa. 2005). Here, the Complaint itself establishes that [the alleged wrong] occurred on [date]. Specifically, at paragraph [X], Plaintiff alleges [direct quote or paraphrase establishing the triggering event]. Accordingly, Plaintiff's claims accrued no later than [date]."

**Step 3 — Show the time bar mathematically:**
> "Plaintiff commenced this action on [filing date], as reflected in the docket. Under the applicable [X]-year statute of limitations, Plaintiff was required to file this action by [SOL expiration date]. Plaintiff filed this action [X days/months/years] after the limitations period expired. Plaintiff's claims are therefore time-barred and must be dismissed."

**Step 4 — Address tolling (preemptively):**
> "The Complaint contains no allegations supporting any recognized tolling doctrine. Plaintiff has not alleged fraudulent concealment, equitable tolling, or any other basis to excuse the untimely filing. Bare assertions of tolling are insufficient under the plausibility standard of *Twombly* and *Iqbal*. *Cunningham v. M&T Bank Corp.*, [cite] (3d Cir. 2016)."

**Step 5 — Preempt the discovery rule:**
> "To the extent Plaintiff might invoke Pennsylvania's discovery rule, the Complaint forecloses that argument. Pennsylvania's discovery rule tolls the limitations period only where 'the plaintiff is reasonably unaware of her injury or its cause.' *Fine v. Checcio*, 870 A.2d 850, 859 (Pa. 2005). The rule is 'employed only for worthy cases' and 'cannot be applied so loosely as to nullify the purpose for which a statute of limitations exists.' *Dalrymple v. Brown*, 701 A.2d 164, 167 (Pa. 1997). Here, the Complaint's own allegations establish that Plaintiff [knew / discovered / was put on notice of] [the injury and its cause] as of [date]. Paragraph [X] alleges [specific allegation showing knowledge]. Accordingly, even under the discovery rule, Plaintiff's claims accrued no later than [date] — still more than [X years] before this action was filed."

**Step 6 — Futility of amendment:**
> "Dismissal with prejudice is warranted because leave to amend would be futile. The passage of time cannot be remedied by amendment, and no re-pleading of the Complaint could render Plaintiff's claims timely. *Foman v. Davis*, 371 U.S. 178, 182 (1962) (leave to amend properly denied when amendment would be futile)."

## III. Claim-Specific Accrual Rules and SOL Periods

| Claim | SOL | Accrual Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Negligence / personal injury | 2 years (§ 5524) | Date of injury; or discovery (if latent) |
| Breach of written contract | 4 years (§ 5525) | Date of breach (not discovery) |
| Fraud / misrepresentation | 2 years (§ 5524(7)) | Discovery of fraud (discovery rule applies) |
| § 1983 civil rights | 2 years (borrowed) | Date plaintiff knew or should have known of injury and who caused it |
| Medical malpractice | 2 years (§ 5524(2)) | Discovery of negligent act and its causal relation |
| Wrongful death | 2 years (§ 5524) | Date of death |

## IV. Preempting Discovery Rule Arguments — Key Phrases

- "Even crediting Plaintiff's discovery rule allegations, paragraph [X] establishes Plaintiff had [actual knowledge / constructive notice] as of [date]."
- "Plaintiff cannot invoke the discovery rule where the Complaint's own allegations show Plaintiff [consulted counsel / received diagnosis / experienced the alleged harm] on [date]."
- "The discovery rule is not a general tolling provision; it applies only where the injury is 'unknowable' to a reasonably diligent plaintiff. Nothing in the Complaint supports that conclusion."
- "Plaintiff's own pleading 'locks' the accrual date. Under FRCP 12(b)(6), the Court accepts all well-pleaded allegations as true. The Complaint establishes knowledge/accrual by [date]."

## V. Conclusion Block (Standard)

Plaintiff's claims are time-barred under the applicable Pennsylvania statute of limitations, the time-bar is apparent on the face of the Complaint, and no amendment could cure this fundamental defect."
